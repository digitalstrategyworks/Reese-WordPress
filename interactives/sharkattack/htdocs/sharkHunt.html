Sharks on the Hunt...

The Great White is the shark species most responsible for attacks on humans, yet most scientists would attribute these attacks to misidentification of prey. In most cases, the shark will release the human after realizing that it is not a seal or fish part of its natural diet. Unfortunately, just a test bite from these massive beasts is enough to fatally wound a human. The diagram below shows the anatomy of a Great White and how it hunts.

A Great White has rows of serrated teeth, and uses them to bite and tear prey apart. Sharks, unique from other animals, also has the ability to move both the top and bottom jaws, which gives the beast incredible power and force when devouring victims.
A lateral line is a unique sense that allows a shark to easily detect prey through their vibrations in the water. Special pores called Ampullae of Lorenzini allow sharks to sense electromagnetic fields created by movements of their prey in the water.
Sharks have an acute sense of directional smell. Studies indicate that sharks also have impeccable hearing that allows them to hear sounds from great distances, giving them the ability to track the distressed sounds of wounded prey for miles.
Great Whites will use muscular force and momentum to attack prey from behind or below with one lethal strike. Most sharks of this species weigh between 1,500 and 2,400 pounds.
